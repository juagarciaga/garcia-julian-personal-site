## Research Highlights

### 🤖 AI & Multi-agent Systems

Game theory provides a natural framework for understanding and designing AI agents that must coordinate, compete, or cooperate with others. We use evolutionary game theory and reinforcement learning to study how cooperation can emerge in decentralised multi-agent systems.

**Learning to cooperate against ensembles of diverse opponents** [[Journal]](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00521-024-10511-9)
Isuri Perera, Frits de Nijs and <u>Julian García</u>. **Neural Computing and Applications**, 2025.

How can an RL agent learn to cooperate when facing many different, anonymous opponents? Inspired by evolutionary game theory, we design agents that maintain cooperation across diverse partners without needing to model each one individually.

**Cooperation and Reputation Dynamics with Reinforcement Learning** [[Proceedings]](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.5555/3463952.3463972)
Nicolas Anastassacos, <u>Julian García</u>, Stephen Hailes and Mirco Musolesi. **AAMAS**, 2021.

Can RL agents independently learn to use reputation to sustain cooperation? We show they can — but only with the right nudges. A combination of seeding and intrinsic rewards based on altruistic reciprocation stabilises cooperation even in fully decentralised settings.

**Using Evolutionary Game Theory to Understand Scalability in Task Allocation** [[Proceedings]](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3520304.3529073)
Mostafa Rizk, <u>Julian García</u>, Aldeida Aleti and David Green. **GECCO**, 2022.

We compare centralised and decentralised approaches to multi-agent task allocation using neuroevolution. A replicator dynamics model reveals that the geometry of physical coordination — not the learning architecture — is the main bottleneck for scalability.

### 🧬 Social Dynamics

Human societies face collective action problems everywhere — from public goods to political debate. We use game theory and simulations to understand how cooperation, punishment, and social norms emerge and break down.

**Cooperative Dilemmas in Rational Debate** [[arXiv]](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.06485)
Toby Handfield, <u>Julian García</u>, Christian Hilbe and Shang Long Yeo. **arXiv preprint**, 2025.

Rational debate requires cooperation — everyone benefits from reaching true beliefs, but nobody wants to be the one to change their mind. We show that this tension creates social dilemmas where individually optimal strategies undermine collective truth-seeking.

**Evolution of coordinated punishment to enforce cooperation from an unbiased strategy space** [[Journal]](https://royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rsif.2019.0127)
<u>Julian García</u> and Arne Traulsen. **Journal of the Royal Society Interface**, 2019.

Most models of punishment restrict which strategies are available. We include all possible strategies — including anti-social ones — and find that coordinated pro-social institutions can still emerge, but only when individuals can observe and condition on whether such institutions exist.

**Direct reciprocity in structured populations** [[Journal]](https://www.pnas.org/content/109/25/9929.abstract)
Matthijs van Veelen, <u>Julian García</u>, David G. Rand and Martin A. Nowak. **PNAS**, 2012.

Direct reciprocity alone does not lead to high cooperation in well-mixed populations. But combine repeated interactions with even a small amount of population structure, and conditional cooperation thrives. Featured in a [perspective in Science](https://science.sciencemag.org/content/337/6100/1304).

### 🎲 Game Theory

We work on foundational questions in evolutionary game theory — how strategies evolve in repeated interactions, what role mutation and strategy selection play, and why equilibrium results can be misleading.

**Picking strategies in games of cooperation** [[Journal]](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.2319925121)
<u>Julian García</u> and Arne Traulsen. **PNAS**, 2025.

How modellers choose which strategies to include in evolutionary game theory models can quietly shape the conclusions. We propose principles for more systematic strategy selection and argue that AI methods can help build richer models of cooperation.

**Repeated games with partner choice** [[Journal]](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pcbi.1012810)
Christopher Graser, Takako Fujiwara-Greve, <u>Julian García</u> and Matthijs van Veelen. **PLOS Computational Biology**, 2025.

What happens when players can walk away and find a new partner? The option to leave actually increases cooperation — people end up choosing dependable partners, creating a form of assortment that standard repeated game models miss.

**No strategy can win in the repeated prisoner's dilemma** [[Journal]](https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/frobt.2018.00102/full)
<u>Julian García</u> and Matthijs van Veelen. **Frontiers in Robotics and AI**, 2018.

For every Nash equilibrium in the repeated prisoner's dilemma, there are sequences of mutants that destabilise it. Populations cycle between equilibria with different levels of cooperation — and this instability is inescapable regardless of how strategies are represented.

### 🔬 Science of Science

Scientific institutions — journals, peer review, public communication — are themselves strategic systems. We apply game theory to understand how incentives shape the quality of science and its public understanding.

**Academic journals, incentives, and the quality of peer review: a model** [[Journal]](https://doi.org/10.1017/psa.2023.81)
Kevin J. S. Zollman, <u>Julian García</u> and Toby Handfield. **Philosophy of Science**, 2023.

We model how different incentives shape journal behaviour in peer review. When journals chase high rejection rates rather than quality, they are incentivised to make review less accurate — leading to worse papers being published.

**A technocognitive approach to detecting fallacies in climate misinformation** [[Journal]](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-024-76139-w)
Francisco Zanartu, John Cook, Markus Wagner and <u>Julian García</u>. **Scientific Reports**, 2024.

We train models to detect logical fallacies in climate misinformation, achieving 2.5–3.5x better detection than prior work. Fake experts and anecdotal arguments are easiest to catch; fallacies requiring background knowledge remain harder.
